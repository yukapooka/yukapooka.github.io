Decision Design in Population Genomics and Data Sharing

Considered how governance choices, trust, and data-sharing structures influence participation, access, and downstream decision-making.

Context

Population-scale genomic research depends on data — and lots of it. But as genomic datasets have grown in scientific value, so has the complexity of sharing them. Regulatory frameworks vary across jurisdictions, patient anonymisation standards remain contested, and institutions are increasingly cautious about how their data is accessed and used. The result is a landscape where the scientific potential of large-scale genomic data sharing is real, but the structures needed to unlock it are still being worked out. This case study examines work done to evaluate whether privacy-preserving technologies could offer a viable path toward more secure and trusted genomic data sharing across institutions.

The Problem

The barriers to genomic data sharing are as much about trust and governance as they are about infrastructure. Institutions are willing to participate in collaborative research in principle, but have legitimate concerns about how data is protected, who can access it, and under what conditions. Regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions compounds this, and patient anonymisation remains a sensitive and unresolved challenge across many settings. The scientific case for pooling data is well established. The question was whether there was a technically feasible architecture that could deliver the analytical benefits of data sharing while meaningfully addressing those concerns.

What I Did

I led the reasibility evaluation for applying privacy-preserving technologies to population-scale genomic analysis — assessing whether they could support more secure and trusted data sharing across institutions. To ensure the evaluation was grounded in real-world conditions, the POC was structured around publicly available population genomic data and published research questions with known outputs — so that results could be benchmarked against established findings rather than evaluated in isolation. Both privacy-preserving approaches were stress-tested against real analytical tasks, and the governance considerations that any viable architecture would need to address were mapped alongside the technical ones.

The Outcome

The POC demonstrated that privacy-preserving approaches were technically feasible for genomic analysis tasks at population scale — producing results consistent with published benchmarks when validated against UK Biobank data and established research questions. The work established a concrete requirements baseline — technical and governance — for what a privacy-preserving approach to genomic data sharing would need to address to be viable in practice.

What This Illustrates

Data sharing in healthcare is not primarily a technical problem. The hardest constraints are governance and trust, and designing a platform that addresses them requires treating those constraints as product requirements, not just as context. In regulated, high-stakes environments, the architecture of trust is as important as the architecture of the system itself.
