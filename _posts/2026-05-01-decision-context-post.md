*AI products are often discussed as if they were simply better software. But once outputs influence human judgment under uncertainty, product thinking must account for trust, misuse, override, and the uneven consequences of error.*
<br />

---
<br />

### Opening Tension
<p>AI products are often discussed as if they were simply software products with a more powerful backend. But the core challenge is different: their outputs shape human decisions under uncertainty, so product quality depends on how those outputs are interpreted and used in context.</p>
<br />


### Why AI Product Development Is Different
<p>AI outputs are probabilistic and context-dependent, which means conventional software assumptions about specification, execution, and testing do not fully carry over.</p>
<br />


### What the Evidence Shows
<p>User bias, interpretability, uncertainty, workflow fit, and human factors all shape whether AI systems work well in practice (Kostick-Quenet, Tomsett, Knop/Tun).</p>
<br />


### What This Looks Like in Healthcare
<p>Healthcare makes the product problem easier to see because real use depends on transparency, training, usability, workflow integration, autonomy, and outcomes in practice (Tun, Knop, Wilhelm).</p>
<br />


### Where Software Product Thinking Falls Short
<p>Inherited software instincts underweight uncertainty communication, acceptable error thresholds, human override, and post-deployment adaptation.</p>
<br />


### Why Responsible AI Has to Be a Product Discipline
<p>Responsible AI should influence scoping, rollout, measurement, monitoring, and incident response, not just model review or compliance (NIST, OECD, Wilhelm).</p>

### Closing Takeaway
<p>The challenge of AI products is not only intelligence. It is designing for judgment: building systems whose outputs can be interpreted, questioned, integrated into workflows, and monitored responsibly once they are used in the real world.</p>
