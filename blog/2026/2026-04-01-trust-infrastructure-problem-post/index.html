When the stakes are high, trust depends less on what a model can do than on the infrastructure around it: evidence, oversight, accountability, implementation, and long-term stewardship.


Opening Tension

High-stakes systems are often judged first by whether they work. But in practice, trust depends on more than technical performance. It depends on whether the surrounding infrastructure makes the system governable, accountable, and dependable over time.


Why Trust Breaks in High-Stakes Systems

Trust breaks when institutions cannot clearly explain evaluation standards, accountability, data handling, or post-deployment oversight.


What the Evidence Shows

Trust in high-stakes settings depends on governance, implementation capacity, and demonstrated trustworthiness rather than claims alone (Milne, O’Doherty, Hogg).


What This Looks Like in Healthcare

Healthcare makes infrastructure visible because oversight, organizational readiness, and data governance directly affect whether technical systems become acceptable in practice (Kerasidou, Nong, Hogg).


What Infrastructure Actually Builds Trust

Trust is built through governance functions, stewardship, accountability, and credible operating arrangements (O’Doherty, OECD).


Implications for Builders and Institutions

Trustworthy high-stakes systems require more than strong models: they need evaluation pathways, oversight bodies, governance routines, data stewardship, and implementation support.

Closing Takeaway

In high-stakes systems, trust is not a feature that can be claimed at launch. It is an infrastructural achievement that has to be built, maintained, and demonstrated over time.
