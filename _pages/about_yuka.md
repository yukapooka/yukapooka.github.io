I started in computational biology and bioinformatics, working on problems at the intersection of data, systems, and healthcare. Over time, I found myself increasingly drawn not only to the technical analysis itself, but to the decisions, workflows, and coordination around it: how complex technical outputs are interpreted, acted on, and integrated into real-world settings.

Across healthcare and biotech environments, much of my work evolved toward product-shaped and systems-level problems. I was often most engaged when the challenge involved clarifying complexity, improving how work moved across people and systems, or helping technical efforts become more usable, actionable, and operationally meaningful.

As I worked more closely with predictive models, analytics, and decision-support contexts, I became increasingly interested in a deeper question: not just whether a system performs well technically, but how it shapes human judgment. In high-stakes environments, model performance alone is not enough. Trust calibration, workflow design, overreliance, uncertainty, and institutional context all influence whether a system actually improves decisions.

That interest led me to deepen my focus in Responsible AI and behavioral decision science, particularly around the design of human-AI systems that support decision quality rather than simply optimize model outputs. I’m especially interested in the gap between predictive accuracy and real-world usefulness: the point where interface design, governance, workflow structure, and human behavior all begin to matter.

Although my titles were not formally PM or TPM titles, many of the problems I worked on required the same underlying skills: problem framing, technical translation, stakeholder alignment, prioritization, and structured execution. In practice, I often worked at the intersection of technical depth, cross-functional coordination, and strategic decision-making.

What draws me now to healthcare product, Responsible AI, decision science, and platform strategy work is the combination of complexity and consequence. These are environments where the challenge is not simply building something powerful, but ensuring that it is usable, trusted, well-integrated, and supportive of thoughtful human judgment.

My background gives me a strong foundation in technical and scientific environments, but the work I want to keep doing sits more broadly across product, decision systems, governance, and execution. I’m particularly interested in healthcare PM, TPM, product strategy, innovation strategy, Responsible AI, and decision science or behavioral science roles where technical depth, cross-functional clarity, and long-term systems thinking matter.

<h4>How I work</h4>

<p>I tend to do my best work in environments where technical depth and practical decision-making need to come together. I’m motivated by problems that require more than analysis alone — the kind where progress depends on clearer framing, better coordination, stronger workflow design, more deliberate tradeoff-making, or a better understanding of how people actually use systems in practice.

In teams, I naturally gravitate toward making complexity easier to work with. That might mean structuring a messy problem, translating between technical and operational stakeholders, identifying what actually matters, or helping a new effort move from idea to operational reality.
